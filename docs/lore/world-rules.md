# World Rules

The physics and politics. Use these to keep new content consistent.

## The shape of the sky

Three kinds of world, by the Union's reckoning. The split is what makes the Quiet Law make sense and what will make S2 start to break.

- **The silent worlds.** Pre-stellar. Have not left their own star. The vast majority. Subjects of the Quiet Law: no contact, no exceptions. *These are who Kalen talks to.*
- **The known starflight powers.** Civilisations that crossed the FTL bar and announced themselves. The Union is one. There are a handful of others — diplomatic contact, treaties, the occasional hostile silence. They are listed by name in the Compact's index.
- **The hidden starflight powers.** Civilisations that crossed the FTL bar and did *not* announce themselves. Almost certainly aware the Union exists; they have chosen not to be aware in return. The Union assumes there are some. The Union does not know how many.

A silent world that crosses the FTL bar **always** joins one of the two upper categories — revealed or hidden. The Union has never seen one cross and then stay quiet without being hidden on purpose. This is the bar the Quiet Law was written against: *what a young civilisation does once it can hear us.*

### The threat model the Union actually carries

Other starflight powers are the only real existential pressure the Union plans for. Silent worlds are seen the way teachers see children: *they will grow up, they will not be a threat for a long time, they may eventually be peers.* The danger lives at the top tier. Most Union strategic planning is keyed to **the known starflight powers** and to the small, paranoid hedge that **the list may be incomplete.**

> "We think we know all of them." — the most-quoted line in the Compact's threat appendix. It is not phrased as confidence. It is phrased as a warning. *(S2 hook: the line was right to be a warning.)*

## The Union

A post-scarcity alliance of 60+ worlds. Old enough that no living citizen remembers a pre-Union era. Old enough that *most things are already decided*.

### What it feels like
- Stable. Functional. Bureaucratic.
- Materially comfortable, spiritually flat.
- The ruling apparatus is committee-shaped, not throne-shaped. Decisions take years. Vetoes are easy. New laws are rare; old laws compound.
- The Union is **not** evil and **not** utopian. It is a working civilisation that has chosen quiet survival over heroic gesture.

### Structure (only the names that show on screen)
- **The Compact** — the founding accord. Cited by lawyers and protesters with equal reverence.
- **The Quiet Law** — the prohibition on contact (see below). The most-litigated clause in Union history.
- **Communications Defense (ComDef)** — Sera's branch. Hunts unauthorised emissions inside Union space. Small, severe, well-funded.
- **The Listening Service** — does the *legal* version of what Kalen does. Watches, never speaks.
- **Border Wardens** — patrol the edge between Union space and silent worlds. Their job is to stop Kalen and people like him.

## The Quiet Law

> **No contact with worlds before they can leave their own star.**

The single clause. Everything else is enforcement.

### Why it exists
History. Across the Union's first three centuries, recorded contacts with pre-stellar worlds produced:
- 41 civilisational collapses (religious or political)
- 12 technological cascades into self-destruction (a culture handed an idea it could not yet contain)
- 7 cases of total cultural dissolution (the contacted world abandons its own identity)
- 0 *unambiguous* net-positive contacts on the record.

The Law is therefore framed as **harm-reduction**, not virtue. It is taught in school the way modern Earth teaches seatbelts.

The phrasing — *on the record* — does work the Compact does not own up to. The Listening Service has, quietly, observed a handful of post-contact worlds that *prospered*: contacts that landed well, where the receiving culture absorbed the signal without breaking. None are in the dataset the Quiet Law cites. The official defence is that none can be *unambiguously* attributed to deliberate contact rather than coincidence. The unofficial reading, which Sera will eventually voice, is that good outcomes were filtered out so the Law could be taught cleanly. Kalen's own log contains a small number of these — flukes of timing, restraint, and luck. Ep 7 makes the filtering explicit.

### How it's argued against
Three steady critiques the show should give airtime:
1. **The cost of silence is not zero.** Worlds suffer. Plagues, famines, extinctions. The Law is calm about that suffering.
2. **The criterion is arbitrary.** "Can leave their star" is one bar. Why not literacy? Why not peace? Whoever picked the bar wrote the law.
3. **It assumes the Union knows best.** A polity that has stopped growing tells younger polities they may not grow either.

Kalen believes (1) and (2). Sera, in her better moments, fears (3).

## Physics & technology

### What works
- **Faster-than-light travel.** Real but expensive: a single FTL jump consumes a small moon's worth of energy. Used for couriers, freight, and the highest-priority diplomatic missions. Not for chatting.
- **FTL relays.** A fixed grid of stations stretched across Union space, each one a power-hungry beacon. Signals on the grid travel near-instantly between adjacent nodes. **This grid is where the hidden hijack-system lives** — Kalen never knew.
- **Sub-light comms.** Cheap, slow. Most inter-world traffic is on these. Decade-long round-trip latencies are normal and budgeted around.
- **Quantum cryptography** is mature; *intelligence on whether a message was sent* is not. Hence ComDef must hunt emissions physically.
- **Materials engineering** is far ahead of contemporary Earth — diamond-class lattices, room-temp superconductors — but the look stays **practical and analog**. Cabling is visible. Switches click.

### What doesn't work
- No matter teleportation. No transporters.
- No energy weapons that ignore physics. Sidearms exist. They are slug-throwers with smart payloads. Firefights are short and ugly.
- No mind-reading. No psionics. No magical "the AI just solves it" — AIs in the Union are competent tools, not deities, and Union law treats them as tools deliberately.
- No time travel. Causality is sacred.

### Communication lag *matters*
Make a contact with a world thirty light-years away on sub-light tools? You hear back in sixty years. Most of Kalen's contacts are therefore on the **FTL relay grid** (he abuses official infrastructure to talk to silent worlds), or he uses local astronomical phenomena (auroras, ionosphere, seismic) on worlds that already have his signal in the air.

## How Kalen actually contacts worlds

He does not have a ship. He does not have a transporter. He has the relay grid, a clever mind, and a closet full of out-of-date analog tools. Methods used across S1:

| Method | Example episode | What it physically does |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Radio leakage** | Ep 1 (Desert) | Push an FTL signal sideways into a world's existing radio spectrum — they hear it on their own primitive receivers. |
| **Seismic resonance** | Ep 2 (Sea Choir) | Modulate a tight pulse train against ocean-floor crust; their hydrophones interpret it as voice. |
| **Aurora modulation** | Ep 3 (Sky Language) | Drive charged particles into the upper atmosphere in patterns that hand-decode as text. |
| **Magnetic storms** | Ep 4 (Fire Given) | Encode bursts in geomagnetic field perturbations; they read it via compass anomalies. |
| **Deep-ocean sound** | Ep 5 (Perfect Garden) | Long-range hydroacoustic channels in the SOFAR layer. |
| **Moon reflection** | (recurring) | Bounce a tight beam off a moon they already track; their telescopes log it as anomaly. |
| **Old satellites** | (recurring) | Wake their own dormant equipment and ventriloquise through it. |
| **Hacked scientific gear** | Ep 7 (Echoes) | Push a payload through their own colliders / observatories. |
| **Accidental signal bleed from FTL relays** | Ep 8 (Finale) | The thing he *thought* was his own ingenuity — turns out the relays leak. And someone is *amplifying* the leak. |

All of these methods will appear as **gamble upgrades** in the game. See [`game-mapping.md`](./game-mapping.md).

## Factions on the political board (use sparingly)

- **The Patient** — Union mainstream. The Quiet Law is sacred. Even questioning it is suspect.
- **The Hand** — pragmatic centrists. The Law is right but not eternal; some worlds *should* be contacted, *carefully*. Sera leans Hand.
- **The Open** — small, persecuted, sometimes violent. Information must be free. Kalen would call himself Open if pressed.
- **The Listeners** — clerical-academic order that runs the Listening Service. They believe contact is sin but watching is sacred duty.

Most ordinary citizens are Patient by default and Hand on Sunday.

## What's silent (and shouldn't be filled in yet)

Hold these blank — they pay off in S2/S3:
- Who built the FTL relay grid. (The Union claims it built the grid. The grid is older than the Union.)
- Whether the hijack-system is *one* thing or *many*.
- Whether the silent worlds being accelerated are being *prepared* (welcomed) or *fattened* (preyed on).
- The identities and number of the known starflight powers. (Coin a name only if a scene needs it, and only after writers'-room sign-off. The "we think we know all of them" line in the threat appendix is doing more work than its surface.)
- Whether the **hidden** starflight powers are one thing or many, and whether one of them is the destination the hijack-system has been seeding worlds *toward*. (S2 territory.)

Until we know in the writers' room, the game must not commit to an answer.

## S2 hook — the recount

S1 closes on the broad pattern: silent worlds being lit up too early, too fast, too loud. S2 begins with the Union doing the count it has not done in a century: walking every known starflight power on the list and asking, *are you doing this?* The answers come back *no*, in every direction. Which means one of two things:

- Someone on the list is lying. *(Less interesting; doable.)*
- The list is short by at least one. *(The intended read. The line in the threat appendix was right to be a warning.)*

S2 protagonists are Sera + a Listening Service archivist; Kalen is on the bench. The investigation finds a starflight power that has been hidden long enough that the Union no longer remembers it was hidden *from*. That power is the one the hijack-system has been talking to.

## S3 hook — Kalen's army

The "young worlds reaching outward" from the S1 finale are not, in S3, a wavefront of victims. They are recipients of a signal they answered, and *they remember the voice that taught them how to answer.* Hundreds of contacted civilisations, accelerated to FTL on a decade-class compression of the natural curve, find each other across the relay grid and find a common origin: a single, recurring carrier signature buried in the modulation. *Kalen's.*

This makes Kalen, against his will and the Union's, the convening figure of a polity that is not the Union, was never asked to exist, and is now too loud to ignore. S3 is the question of what he does with that, and whether he can refuse it. The working name for this polity in the writers' room is **the Echo Compact**. Do not use the name in S1 or S2 copy.

Hold blank in S1 game copy: the *existence* of this future. S1's job is to make the player understand that Kalen has been heard. S3's job is to reveal who answered.
